1 Introduction

The rapid development of artificial intelligence (AI) over the past few years has fundamentally transformed how technology is embedded in daily life, reshaping industries, communication practices, and public discourse (Stone et al., 2022). As AI evolved from a niche technical field into a widely discussed social phenomenon, public opinion surrounding it has expanded significantly across digital platforms. Social media, particularly Twitter (now X), has played a central role in this transformation by providing a space where users, individual founders, and institutions actively discuss, evaluate, and negotiate the meaning and implications of AI technologies (Bruns & Highfield, 2015). Given this context, Twitter data offers a valuable lens for understanding how people’s perception and opinions toward AI develop over time, especially during periods of rapid technology progress. Analyzing such discourse through computational social data analysis is particularly meaningful for the social science field, enabling researchers to systematically examine how technological innovation is interpreted, debated, negotiated, and culturally integrated within society.

Despite growing scholarly attention to AI-related communication, relatively few studies have examined how public discourse evolves longitudinally alongside the mainstream adoption of AI tools (Cave et al., 2019). Most existing research tends to focus on public perceptions or attitudes measured through surveys or short-term datasets (Cave et al., 2019), which limits the ability to capture dynamic shifts in attention, framing, and emotional response over time. To address this gap, this study investigates AI-related discourse on Twitter between 2017 and 2021, a period during which the application of AI tools was significantly expanded. By analyzing large-scale tweet data, this research seeks to identify how thematic structures and sentiment patterns change across time and how these changes reflect broader socio-cultural shifts.

Accordingly, this study is guided by two research questions:

  1. How did the core themes and topics of AI-related discourse on Twitter evolve between 2017 and 2021 as the technology moved toward mainstream adoption?

  2. How are these identified themes and specific sentiment polarities associated with user engagement (e.g., likes, replies, and retweets)?

2 Data

2.1 Dataset

To address the questions, this study used a curated and anonymized dataset with large-scaled AI-related tweets collected via the Twitter API from 2017 to 2021, including metadata such as engagement metrics, hashtags, language, and temporal features, supporting research on public discourse, sentiment, and topic evolution (Marcellis-Warin et al., 2025). The unit of analysis for this study is each individual tweet with its associated metadata.

2.4 Descriptive Statistics of Engagement Metrics

Table 2 shows the descriptive statistics of the numeric variables. As a supplement, Table 3 shows the count and proportion of zero and non-zero values among these numeric variables.

The descriptive statistics across all three engagement metrics (counts of likes, replies, and retweets) show a highly skewed distribution, indicating that the majority of tweets in this dataset received no engagement. Among the metrics, likes are the most common form of interaction, with a mean of 3.04 and 40.40% of tweets receiving at least one like, followed closely by retweets at a mean of 2.35 and 41.40% non-zero values. Replies were notably rare, with only 7.07% of tweets receiving any response and a mean of just 0.14. The relatively large standard deviations, combined with the substantial gaps between the 75th percentile values (0 or 1) and the maximum values (898 likes, 83 replies, 367 retweets), suggest the presence of a small number of highly viral tweets that disproportionately inflate the averages.

2.5 Date Variable

The date variable involved in this study is date_utc, derived from the original created_at variable and normalized to UTC (see Appendix for the conversion process). It ranges from January 27th, 2017, to July 19th, 2021, covering a total time span of 5 years (1,634 days).

The unit of analysis over time in this study is each day. Figure 1 shows the daily distribution of tweets, where the x-axis displays the time range of the dataset (January 27th, 2017, to July 19th, 2021) and the y-axis represents the counts of observed tweets by day. Overall, tweet volume remained relatively low and stable in the earlier years of the dataset, generally fluctuating between 0 and 20 tweets per day, before showing a notable increase in activity around 2020–2021, with several prominent spikes, including one approaching 100 tweets in a single day, suggesting a growing public interest in AI-related discourse toward the end of the observation period.

2.7 Text Preparation

Text cleaning and tokenization are performed as a preprocessing step for subsequent analyses.

Code 
# text cleaning
data <- data |>
  mutate(textBU = text) |>           # back up raw text before cleaning
  filter(!is.na(text)) |>
  mutate(
    text = textutils::HTMLdecode(text),           # decode HTML entities (e.g., &amp;)
    text = str_to_lower(text),                    # normalize to lowercase
    text = str_remove_all(text, "https?://\\S+"), # strip URLs
    text = str_remove_all(text, "\\d+"),          # strip digits
    text = str_remove_all(text, "[@]\\w+"),       # strip @mentions
    text = str_squish(text)                        # collapse extra whitespace
  ) |>
  mutate(
    tweet_index = row_number(),           # sequential ID for joining
    nwords      = str_count(text, "\\w+") # word count after cleaning
  )

3 Methods

3.1 Statistical Methods

Prior to model estimation, correlation analysis is conducted as an exploratory step to examine the pairwise relationships between thematic features, sentiment scores, and user engagement metrics. This preliminary analysis helps identify potential associations and informs the selection of variables for subsequent modeling, while also providing an initial understanding of the direction and strength of relationships within the dataset.

In the meantime, this paper employs Generalized Linear Models (GLMs) to generate models to examine the relationship between variables. GLMs were chosen over simple linear regression due to the nature of the engagement data containing a preponderance of zero values, which is a distributional characteristic that standard linear models may not adequately handle. By accommodating non-normal response distributions, GLMs offer a more flexible and appropriate modeling approach for count-based social media engagement data.

3.2 Text-as-Data Methods

For text analysis, this paper draws on two complementary computational techniques. Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) topic modeling, which is a widely used unsupervised machine learning technique, is applied to uncover potential thematic structures within the dataset, identifying the core subjects that characterized AI-related discourse. Meanwhile, sentiment analysis with lexicons, a natural language processing approach evaluating the emotional tone of textual data by leveraging predefined lexical resources to classify polarity (e.g., positive, negative, or neutral), is also employed to establish the overall emotional polarity and trends among tweets. In addition, Term Frequency-Inverse Document Frequency (TF-IDF) is used to track how the salience of key AI-related terms shifted from 2017 to 2021, offering a longitudinal perspective on lexical change as AI moved toward mainstream adoption.

These methods directly support the research questions investigating how identified themes and sentiments polarities are associated with user engagement, as Topic Modeling and Sentiment Analysis allow the study to identify underlying themes and sentiments, while TF-IDF provides a temporal perspective on the evolving trends of AI-related key terms across the dataset. Together, these techniques inform the subsequent inferential analysis, in which GLM provide insights into the predictive relationships between textual variables and engagement metrics statistically.

4.1 LDA Topic Modeling

To identify the underlying thematic structure of AI-related tweets, LDA was applied to the preprocessed tweet corpus. After tokenization, tweets were filtered using a custom stopword list that removed general English stopwords, Twitter-specific terms (e.g., "rt", "tweet"), and the search keyword "artificial intelligence". A TF-IDF filter then retained only words appearing in more than 0.1% but fewer than 75% of all documents, reducing vocabulary noise while preserving informative terms. The resulting document-term matrix served as input to the LDA model, which was estimated with K = 5 topics using Gibbs sampling with 2,000 iterations and a 500-iteration burn-in period.

4.4.2 Sentiment and Topic as Predictors

To directly test the associations between discourse characteristics and engagement, three negative binomial GLMs were fitted predicting likes, replies, and retweets from AFINN sentiment scores, LDA topic probabilities (Topics 1–4, with Topic 5 as the implicit baseline through the compositional constraint), and year fixed effects (Table 13, Figure 10).

AFINN sentiment was not a statistically significant predictor of any engagement outcome. Its IRR was 0.99 for likes (p = .676), 0.92 for replies (p = .081), and 0.98 for retweets (p = .383). This finding indicates that a more positive or more negative emotional tone alone did not reliably increase or decrease engagement once topic composition and year were taken into account.

By contrast, year was a consistent and substantial predictor of engagement. Relative to 2017, tweets from 2018 showed dramatically higher expected likes (IRR = 4.00) and replies (IRR = 8.23), and tweets from 2020 and 2021 also showed significantly higher likes and replies. Retweets were significantly higher in 2018 (IRR = 2.13) and 2021 (IRR = 2.12) relative to 2017. These large year effects suggest that the overall audience for AI content on Twitter grew substantially after 2017, leading to more engagement across the board regardless of the content’s tone.

Topic effects were present for some outcomes but should be interpreted carefully because LDA topic probabilities are compositional—increasing one topic’s probability necessarily reduces the others. Topic 2 showed a strong negative association with likes and retweets, while Topic 4 showed a strong positive association with replies. However, some estimated IRRs were extremely large or small, indicating instability likely due to near-collinearity among topic predictors. The most defensible interpretation is that business-innovation framing (Topic 4) may be associated with more conversational engagement, while certain technology-and-market content (Topic 2) may attract less passive amplification, but these conclusions require further testing with more robust specifications.

5 Discussion & Conclusion

5.1 Discussion

Taken together, the results provide partial support for both research questions while revealing important nuances in how AI discourse evolved and how it connected to audience engagement on Twitter between 2017 and 2021.

5.1.1 Thematic Stability with Gradual Specialization (RQ1)

For RQ1, the LDA topic modeling results indicate that AI discourse on Twitter did not undergo a dramatic thematic transformation during the study period. Instead, the corpus showed a relatively stable thematic structure built around five recurring clusters: robotics and automation, technology and markets, data science and security, business and innovation, and applied AI ecosystem vocabulary. The near-constant average topic probabilities across all years (all close to 0.20) suggest a diverse but consistently balanced conversation that absorbed new developments without being reorganized by them.

This finding may appear surprising given that 2017 to 2021 saw substantial milestones in AI, including major advances in natural language processing, growing public debate about AI ethics, and the commercialization of generative AI tools. One explanation is that the keyword-based sampling strategy (“Artificial Intelligence”) may have overrepresented promotional, institutional, and hashtag-driven content, which tends to revolve around a stable set of industry frames regardless of specific technological developments. A corpus built around event-specific terms or date-anchored keywords might reveal sharper thematic shifts.

Despite the overall thematic stability, the hashtag analysis provided a more nuanced story about discursive evolution. While #artificialintelligence and #ai dominated in every year, the lower-ranked hashtags shifted gradually toward more specialized terms such as #machinelearning, #datascience, and #deeplearning, while broader infrastructure terms like #bigdata and #iot declined in relative prominence. This pattern suggests that AI discourse on Twitter evolved less through wholesale topical replacement and more through increasing specificity, and users began categorizing their AI discussions into more precise technical subfields rather than relying solely on the broad “artificial intelligence” framing.

The sentiment trend reinforced and sharpened this picture. The steady increase in mean AFINN sentiment from 0.33 in 2017 to 1.11 in 2021 indicates that public AI discourse became progressively more positive over the five years. This is consistent with the broader narrative of AI mainstreaming, in which the technology became increasingly framed as a source of opportunity, economic value, and practical innovation rather than as an abstract, uncertain, or threatening technological force. Critically, sentiment did not differ meaningfully across topics, where the slight gap between the most positive (Topic 4: 0.84) and least positive (Topic 5: 0.79) topics was not statistically significant. This indicates that optimism about AI was a property of the time period in which a tweet was posted, not of the specific thematic cluster it belonged to.

5.1.2 Engagement Patterns and the Role of Context (RQ2)

For RQ2, the most consistent finding was that user engagement was shaped more strongly by historical context than by the specific tone or topic of individual tweets. Year was a robust predictor across all three engagement models: tweets from 2018 showed dramatically higher expected likes (IRR = 4.00) and replies (IRR = 8.23) compared to those from 2017, and elevated engagement persisted through 2020 and 2021 for likes and replies, and through 2021 for retweets. This pattern suggests that as AI became a more mainstream cultural and economic topic in subsequent years, the Twitter audience for AI content grew larger and more active.

Sentiment, by contrast, was not a significant predictor of engagement once year and topic composition were included in the model. The IRRs for AFINN sentiment were close to 1.00 across all three outcomes and did not reach statistical significance. For content creators and communications practitioners, this is an important finding: crafting more enthusiastic or more alarmist AI content did not appear to translate automatically into greater audience engagement, at least within the context of this corpus. Instead, engagement was tied more to the broader social and platform context in which content was posted.

The hashtag analysis added a more actionable finding to the engagement story. The number of hashtags per tweet was consistently and positively associated with all three engagement metrics in the regression models, while simple hashtag presence (just having at least one hashtag) was negatively associated with likes and replies once hashtag count was controlled. This compositional pattern suggests that richly tagged tweets tend to situate content within multiple streams of existing attention, making them more discoverable and more likely to be engaged with. In contrast, a minimal or token use of hashtags does not reliably confer these discoverability benefits. This is a more specific finding than a simple “use hashtags” recommendation and suggests that the density of hashtagging matters.

Topic effects on engagement were present in some models but were difficult to interpret cleanly due to the compositional nature of LDA topic probabilities, which creates near-multicollinearity among topic predictors and produces inflated or unstable coefficient estimates. The tentative pattern — with business-innovation framing (Topic 4) associated with more conversational replies, and technology-market framing (Topic 2) associated with less passive amplification — is theoretically plausible. Business- and future-oriented AI language may invite debate and personal response, while more abstract technology-market language may be shared and amplified less. However, these conclusions require more stable model specifications before they can be stated with confidence.

Cashtags were too rare (only 22 tweets) to support any strong engagement claims. Their near-absence confirms that financial-sector AI discussion was peripheral in this corpus, even if a small number of tweets connected AI talk to cryptocurrency or stock market speculation.

5.2 Limitations

Several limitations qualify these conclusions. First, the analysis used a stratified sample of 3,000 tweets from a corpus of over 893,000 observations, so subtle yearly thematic changes may have been dampened by reduced statistical power. Second, the data were collected using the keyword “Artificial Intelligence,” which may overrepresent institutional, promotional, and professionally framed content while underrepresenting informal, critical, or personal AI discussions. Third, lexicon-based sentiment analysis cannot detect sarcasm, mixed sentiment, or context-dependent irony, which are common in social media writing. Fourth, the LDA topic model was sensitive to the broad shared vocabulary in the corpus, which may explain why yearly topic averages remained so similar across years.

5.3 Future Directions

Future research could address these limitations in several ways. Structural topic modeling (STM), which explicitly models topic prevalence as a function of metadata variables such as year or engagement level, could yield more sensitive estimates of thematic change and its relationship to engagement. Using larger yearly strata or the full corpus would provide greater statistical power to detect subtle shifts in AI discourse. Integrating sentence-level or contextual sentiment models, such as transformer-based classifiers trained on social media data, could capture the nuanced and ironic tone of tweets more validly than word-level dictionaries. Finally, future work could examine specific AI milestones (such as the release of GPT-3 in 2020) as natural experiments to test whether particular events produced detectable shifts in the themes or sentiment of AI discourse on Twitter.

5.4 Conclusion

This study finds that AI discourse on Twitter became steadily more positive and increasingly engaging between 2017 and 2021, without a fundamental reorganization of its underlying thematic structure. The evidence suggests that public AI discourse evolved through two primary mechanisms: increasing technical specialization, as users began labeling their tweets with more precise applied subfield hashtags, and growing optimism, as AI became more integrated into business and everyday technology conversation. Engagement with AI content was driven more by the growing public interest in AI over time than by the specific emotional tone or thematic focus of individual tweets. These findings contribute to a broader understanding of how transformative technologies are discursively normalized and adopted within public social media spaces.

# Introduction

The rapid development of artificial intelligence (AI) over the past few years has fundamentally transformed how technology is embedded in daily life, reshaping industries, communication practices, and public discourse [@stone2022]. As AI evolved from a niche technical field into a widely discussed social phenomenon, public opinion surrounding it has expanded significantly across digital platforms. Social media, particularly Twitter (now X), has played a central role in this transformation by providing a space where users, individual founders, and institutions actively discuss, evaluate, and negotiate the meaning and implications of AI technologies [@bruns2015]. Given this context, Twitter data offers a valuable lens for understanding how people's perception and opinions toward AI develop over time, especially during periods of rapid technology progress. Analyzing such discourse through computational social data analysis is particularly meaningful for the social science field, enabling researchers to systematically examine how technological innovation is interpreted, debated, negotiated, and culturally integrated within society.

Despite growing scholarly attention to AI-related communication, relatively few studies have examined how public discourse evolves longitudinally alongside the mainstream adoption of AI tools [@cave2019]. Most existing research tends to focus on public perceptions or attitudes measured through surveys or short-term datasets [@cave2019], which limits the ability to capture dynamic shifts in attention, framing, and emotional response over time. To address this gap, this study investigates AI-related discourse on Twitter between 2017 and 2021, a period during which the application of AI tools was significantly expanded. By analyzing large-scale tweet data, this research seeks to identify how thematic structures and sentiment patterns change across time and how these changes reflect broader socio-cultural shifts.

Accordingly, this study is guided by two research questions:

1. How did the core themes and topics of AI-related discourse on Twitter evolve between 2017 and 2021 as the technology moved toward mainstream adoption?

2. How are these identified themes and specific sentiment polarities associated with user engagement (e.g., likes, replies, and retweets)?

# Methods

## Statistical Methods

Prior to model estimation, correlation analysis is conducted as an exploratory step to examine the pairwise relationships between thematic features, sentiment scores, and user engagement metrics. This preliminary analysis helps identify potential associations and informs the selection of variables for subsequent modeling, while also providing an initial understanding of the direction and strength of relationships within the dataset.

In the meantime, this paper employs Generalized Linear Models (GLMs) to generate models to examine the relationship between variables. GLMs were chosen over simple linear regression due to the nature of the engagement data containing a preponderance of zero values, which is a distributional characteristic that standard linear models may not adequately handle. By accommodating non-normal response distributions, GLMs offer a more flexible and appropriate modeling approach for count-based social media engagement data.

## Text-as-Data Methods

For text analysis, this paper draws on two complementary computational techniques. Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) topic modeling, which is a widely used unsupervised machine learning technique, is applied to uncover potential thematic structures within the dataset, identifying the core subjects that characterized AI-related discourse. Meanwhile, sentiment analysis with lexicons, a natural language processing approach evaluating the emotional tone of textual data by leveraging predefined lexical resources to classify polarity (e.g., positive, negative, or neutral), is also employed to establish the overall emotional polarity and trends among tweets. In addition, Term Frequency-Inverse Document Frequency (TF-IDF) is used to track how the salience of key AI-related terms shifted from 2017 to 2021, offering a longitudinal perspective on lexical change as AI moved toward mainstream adoption.

These methods directly support the research questions investigating how identified themes and sentiments polarities are associated with user engagement, as Topic Modeling and Sentiment Analysis allow the study to identify underlying themes and sentiments, while TF-IDF provides a temporal perspective on the evolving trends of AI-related key terms across the dataset. Together, these techniques inform the subsequent inferential analysis, in which GLM provide insights into the predictive relationships between textual variables and engagement metrics statistically.

# Results

## LDA Topic Modeling

To identify the underlying thematic structure of AI-related tweets, LDA was applied to the preprocessed tweet corpus. After tokenization, tweets were filtered using a custom stopword list that removed general English stopwords, Twitter-specific terms (e.g., `"rt"`, `"tweet"`), and the search keyword `"artificial intelligence"`. A TF-IDF filter then retained only words appearing in more than 0.1% but fewer than 75% of all documents, reducing vocabulary noise while preserving informative terms. The resulting document-term matrix served as input to the LDA model, which was estimated with K = 5 topics using Gibbs sampling with 2,000 iterations and a 500-iteration burn-in period.

```{r lda-tokenization}

mystopwords <- c(
  stop_words$word,
  "comment", "post",
  "twitter", "tweet", "tweets",

  "artificial", "intelligence"

mystopwords <- tolower(unique(mystopwords))
cat("Total stopwords:", length(mystopwords), "\n")

# tokenize
tm_token_data <- data |>
  unnest_tokens(word, text) |>
  anti_join(data.frame(word = mystopwords)) |>
  mutate(nchar = nchar(word)) |>
  filter(nchar > 2) |>
  filter(!grepl("[0-9]{1}", word)) |>
  filter(!grepl("\\W", word))
```

## Sentiment Analysis

### Comparison of Sentiment Lexicons

Sentiment analysis was conducted using three established dictionary lexicons: AFINN, Bing, and NRC. @tbl-lexicon-coverage summarizes their coverage in the dataset. NRC achieved the broadest coverage at 90.0% of tweets scored, followed by Bing at 78.6%, and AFINN at 42.5%. @fig-lexicon-coverage displays these differences visually. Despite its lower coverage, AFINN was selected as the primary sentiment measure for three reasons. First, AFINN provides a continuous intensity scale (–5 to +5) that captures the degree of positive or negative sentiment, making it directly suitable for regression modeling. Second, among the 1,130 tweets scored by all three lexicons, AFINN correlated strongly with Bing (r = .664) and moderately with NRC (r = .510), while Bing and NRC correlated at r = .528, confirming that all three measures captured a consistent underlying signal. Third, the additional tweets scored by NRC did not justify the increased interpretive complexity of a categorical emotion framework for the purposes of this analysis.

### Topic–Sentiment Relationship

To examine whether discourse topics were associated with different emotional tones, average AFINN sentiment was computed for each topic using gamma-weighted scores, where each tweet's contribution to a topic's average sentiment was proportional to its membership probability in that topic. @fig-topic-sentiment presents these weighted averages. Sentiment scores were very similar across all five topics, ranging from 0.79 (Topic 5, AI ecosystem buzzwords) to 0.84 (Topic 4, business and innovation). A weighted linear regression model predicting sentiment from topic membership found no statistically significant differences relative to Topic 1 for any other topic (all p > .80). This result shows that in this corpus, emotional tone did not vary meaningfully across thematic clusters; rather, sentiment varied more consistently over time than across topics.

### Sentiment and Topic as Predictors

To directly test the associations between discourse characteristics and engagement, three negative binomial GLMs were fitted predicting likes, replies, and retweets from AFINN sentiment scores, LDA topic probabilities (Topics 1–4, with Topic 5 as the implicit baseline through the compositional constraint), and year fixed effects (@tbl-sentiment-topic-glm, @fig-topic-irr).

AFINN sentiment was not a statistically significant predictor of any engagement outcome. Its IRR was 0.99 for likes (p = .676), 0.92 for replies (p = .081), and 0.98 for retweets (p = .383). This finding indicates that a more positive or more negative emotional tone alone did not reliably increase or decrease engagement once topic composition and year were taken into account.

By contrast, year was a consistent and substantial predictor of engagement. Relative to 2017, tweets from 2018 showed dramatically higher expected likes (IRR = 4.00) and replies (IRR = 8.23), and tweets from 2020 and 2021 also showed significantly higher likes and replies. Retweets were significantly higher in 2018 (IRR = 2.13) and 2021 (IRR = 2.12) relative to 2017. These large year effects suggest that the overall audience for AI content on Twitter grew substantially after 2017, leading to more engagement across the board regardless of the content's tone.

Topic effects were present for some outcomes but should be interpreted carefully because LDA topic probabilities are compositional—increasing one topic's probability necessarily reduces the others. Topic 2 showed a strong negative association with likes and retweets, while Topic 4 showed a strong positive association with replies. However, some estimated IRRs were extremely large or small, indicating instability likely due to near-collinearity among topic predictors. The most defensible interpretation is that business-innovation framing (Topic 4) may be associated with more conversational engagement, while certain technology-and-market content (Topic 2) may attract less passive amplification, but these conclusions require further testing with more robust specifications.

# Discussion & Conclusion

## Discussion

Taken together, the results provide partial support for both research questions while revealing important nuances in how AI discourse evolved and how it connected to audience engagement on Twitter between 2017 and 2021.

### Thematic Stability with Gradual Specialization (RQ1)

For RQ1, the LDA topic modeling results indicate that AI discourse on Twitter did not undergo a dramatic thematic transformation during the study period. Instead, the corpus showed a relatively stable thematic structure built around five recurring clusters: robotics and automation, technology and markets, data science and security, business and innovation, and applied AI ecosystem vocabulary. The near-constant average topic probabilities across all years (all close to 0.20) suggest a diverse but consistently balanced conversation that absorbed new developments without being reorganized by them.

This finding may appear surprising given that 2017 to 2021 saw substantial milestones in AI, including major advances in natural language processing, growing public debate about AI ethics, and the commercialization of generative AI tools. One explanation is that the keyword-based sampling strategy ("Artificial Intelligence") may have overrepresented promotional, institutional, and hashtag-driven content, which tends to revolve around a stable set of industry frames regardless of specific technological developments. A corpus built around event-specific terms or date-anchored keywords might reveal sharper thematic shifts.

Despite the overall thematic stability, the hashtag analysis provided a more nuanced story about discursive evolution. While #artificialintelligence and #ai dominated in every year, the lower-ranked hashtags shifted gradually toward more specialized terms such as #machinelearning, #datascience, and #deeplearning, while broader infrastructure terms like #bigdata and #iot declined in relative prominence. This pattern suggests that AI discourse on Twitter evolved less through wholesale topical replacement and more through increasing specificity, and users began categorizing their AI discussions into more precise technical subfields rather than relying solely on the broad "artificial intelligence" framing.

The sentiment trend reinforced and sharpened this picture. The steady increase in mean AFINN sentiment from 0.33 in 2017 to 1.11 in 2021 indicates that public AI discourse became progressively more positive over the five years. This is consistent with the broader narrative of AI mainstreaming, in which the technology became increasingly framed as a source of opportunity, economic value, and practical innovation rather than as an abstract, uncertain, or threatening technological force. Critically, sentiment did not differ meaningfully across topics, where the slight gap between the most positive (Topic 4: 0.84) and least positive (Topic 5: 0.79) topics was not statistically significant. This indicates that optimism about AI was a property of the time period in which a tweet was posted, not of the specific thematic cluster it belonged to.

### Engagement Patterns and the Role of Context (RQ2)

For RQ2, the most consistent finding was that user engagement was shaped more strongly by historical context than by the specific tone or topic of individual tweets. Year was a robust predictor across all three engagement models: tweets from 2018 showed dramatically higher expected likes (IRR = 4.00) and replies (IRR = 8.23) compared to those from 2017, and elevated engagement persisted through 2020 and 2021 for likes and replies, and through 2021 for retweets. This pattern suggests that as AI became a more mainstream cultural and economic topic in subsequent years, the Twitter audience for AI content grew larger and more active.

Sentiment, by contrast, was not a significant predictor of engagement once year and topic composition were included in the model. The IRRs for AFINN sentiment were close to 1.00 across all three outcomes and did not reach statistical significance. For content creators and communications practitioners, this is an important finding: crafting more enthusiastic or more alarmist AI content did not appear to translate automatically into greater audience engagement, at least within the context of this corpus. Instead, engagement was tied more to the broader social and platform context in which content was posted.

The hashtag analysis added a more actionable finding to the engagement story. The number of hashtags per tweet was consistently and positively associated with all three engagement metrics in the regression models, while simple hashtag presence (just having at least one hashtag) was negatively associated with likes and replies once hashtag count was controlled. This compositional pattern suggests that richly tagged tweets tend to situate content within multiple streams of existing attention, making them more discoverable and more likely to be engaged with. In contrast, a minimal or token use of hashtags does not reliably confer these discoverability benefits. This is a more specific finding than a simple "use hashtags" recommendation and suggests that the density of hashtagging matters.

Topic effects on engagement were present in some models but were difficult to interpret cleanly due to the compositional nature of LDA topic probabilities, which creates near-multicollinearity among topic predictors and produces inflated or unstable coefficient estimates. The tentative pattern — with business-innovation framing (Topic 4) associated with more conversational replies, and technology-market framing (Topic 2) associated with less passive amplification — is theoretically plausible. Business- and future-oriented AI language may invite debate and personal response, while more abstract technology-market language may be shared and amplified less. However, these conclusions require more stable model specifications before they can be stated with confidence.

Cashtags were too rare (only 22 tweets) to support any strong engagement claims. Their near-absence confirms that financial-sector AI discussion was peripheral in this corpus, even if a small number of tweets connected AI talk to cryptocurrency or stock market speculation.

## Limitations

Several limitations qualify these conclusions. First, the analysis used a stratified sample of 3,000 tweets from a corpus of over 893,000 observations, so subtle yearly thematic changes may have been dampened by reduced statistical power. Second, the data were collected using the keyword "Artificial Intelligence," which may overrepresent institutional, promotional, and professionally framed content while underrepresenting informal, critical, or personal AI discussions. Third, lexicon-based sentiment analysis cannot detect sarcasm, mixed sentiment, or context-dependent irony, which are common in social media writing. Fourth, the LDA topic model was sensitive to the broad shared vocabulary in the corpus, which may explain why yearly topic averages remained so similar across years.

## Future Directions

Future research could address these limitations in several ways. Structural topic modeling (STM), which explicitly models topic prevalence as a function of metadata variables such as year or engagement level, could yield more sensitive estimates of thematic change and its relationship to engagement. Using larger yearly strata or the full corpus would provide greater statistical power to detect subtle shifts in AI discourse. Integrating sentence-level or contextual sentiment models, such as transformer-based classifiers trained on social media data, could capture the nuanced and ironic tone of tweets more validly than word-level dictionaries. Finally, future work could examine specific AI milestones (such as the release of GPT-3 in 2020) as natural experiments to test whether particular events produced detectable shifts in the themes or sentiment of AI discourse on Twitter.

## Conclusion

This study finds that AI discourse on Twitter became steadily more positive and increasingly engaging between 2017 and 2021, without a fundamental reorganization of its underlying thematic structure. The evidence suggests that public AI discourse evolved through two primary mechanisms: increasing technical specialization, as users began labeling their tweets with more precise applied subfield hashtags, and growing optimism, as AI became more integrated into business and everyday technology conversation. Engagement with AI content was driven more by the growing public interest in AI over time than by the specific emotional tone or thematic focus of individual tweets. These findings contribute to a broader understanding of how transformative technologies are discursively normalized and adopted within public social media spaces.
